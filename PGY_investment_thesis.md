*March 2026 | Based on FY2025 audited financials and bottom-up financial model*

## Executive Summary

Pagaya Technologies is an AI-driven financial technology company that operates a proprietary machine-learning network embedded within major banks, fintechs, and lenders across the United States. The platform analyzes consumer credit applications that lending partners would otherwise decline, uses AI to identify creditworthy borrowers missed by traditional underwriting, and facilitates the origination and funding of those loans through a network of institutional investors. In FY2025, Pagaya generated $1.30B in total revenue on $10.5B of network volume, achieved $371M in Adjusted EBITDA, and — for the first time — posted $81M in GAAP net income.

The stock currently trades at approximately $11.49 per share ($948M market cap), which represents a deeply discounted valuation relative to the company's earnings power: 2.6x FY2025 Adjusted EBITDA and 12.4x trailing GAAP P/E. Wall Street consensus targets average ~$30, implying >150% upside. Our bottom-up model, anchored in network volume growth and fee-rate economics, yields the following DCF-implied share prices (fully diluted):

|----------|--------------|--------------|-----------------|-----------------|

| **Base** | **$22.10** | $1.76B | $565M | 6.2% |

The thesis rests on three pillars: (1) the AI network's ability to sustainably expand network volume across personal loans, auto, and point-of-sale verticals; (2) the operating leverage inherent in a platform model where incremental volume flows through at high marginal profitability; and (3) the transition from balance-sheet-intensive funding to an asset-light fee model, which de-risks the business and improves return on equity.

## Company Overview

**What Pagaya does.** Pagaya operates an AI-powered credit decisioning platform that sits within the loan origination workflow of its lending partners. When a consumer applies for a personal loan, auto loan, or point-of-sale financing through a Pagaya partner, and the partner's own underwriting model declines the application, the anonymized data is forwarded via API to Pagaya's AI network. Pagaya's models — trained on over $2.7 trillion in historical application data — evaluate creditworthiness and, where appropriate, approve the loan. The partner retains the customer relationship and earns servicing fees; Pagaya earns fee revenue (AI integration fees and contract fees) and facilitates the sale of the resulting loan to institutional investors through ABS securitizations and forward flow agreements.

**Why partners integrate.** For lending partners, Pagaya is additive: it enables them to approve more applicants (increasing conversion rates and customer satisfaction) without taking on additional credit risk. For institutional investors, Pagaya provides access to diversified consumer credit assets with AI-enhanced selection. For consumers, the platform expands access to credit at competitive rates.

## Key Concepts an Investor Must Understand

### 1. GAAP Revenue vs. Network Volume — The True Scale of the Business

Pagaya's reported GAAP revenue of $1.30B in FY2025 understates the scale of its economic activity. The company facilitated $10.5B in network volume — the total dollar value of loans originated through its AI platform. Revenue is primarily composed of fee revenue from partners (AI integration fees and contract fees) plus interest income from retained loan interests. The key metric for tracking growth is network volume, from which fee revenue is derived at a "take rate" (FRLPC/Volume) that was approximately 4.9% in FY2025, up from 3.2% in FY2023.

This take rate improvement is central to the bull case: as Pagaya's AI models mature and the funding mix shifts toward lower-cost channels (ABS and forward flow vs. on-balance-sheet), the company captures more economics per dollar of volume.

### 2. Fee Revenue Less Production Costs (FRLPC) Is the Core Profit Metric

FRLPC is analogous to gross profit in a traditional business. It equals fee revenue minus production costs (primarily the cost of acquiring and warehousing loans before they are sold to investors). In FY2025, FRLPC was $512M on $10.5B of volume, yielding a 4.9% FRLPC margin — up from 3.2% in FY2023 and 4.2% in FY2024. This margin expansion reflects both improved AI model performance (better credit selection = lower defaults = better ABS pricing) and a shift toward more efficient funding structures.

### 3. The Balance Sheet Risk — Retained Interests and Secured Borrowing

Unlike a pure SaaS platform, Pagaya retains economic exposure to the loans it facilitates. As of FY2025:
- Investments in loans and securities: $945M
- Secured borrowing: $194M
- Long-term debt (senior unsecured notes): $482M
- Exchangeable notes: $149M
- **Total debt: ~$825M**

The $945M in loan investments represents residual interests in ABS trusts and warehoused loans awaiting securitization. This is the primary source of balance sheet risk: if credit performance deteriorates sharply, these investments could suffer impairments. However, the trend is toward reducing retained exposure — in July 2025, Pagaya issued $500M in 8.875% senior unsecured notes to refinance higher-cost secured borrowings, which both lowered the cost of debt by ~200bps and created ~$40M in annualized cash flow savings.

### 4. The Path to Profitability Was Faster Than Expected

Pagaya's GAAP profitability journey has been dramatic:

| Year | GAAP Net Income | Adjusted EBITDA |
|------|----------------|-----------------|
| FY2022 | ($302M) | ($5M) |
| FY2023 | ($128M) | $82M |
| FY2024 | ($401M)* | $210M |
| FY2025 | $81M | $371M |

*FY2024 GAAP loss was inflated by large non-cash charges including share-based compensation, impairments, and fair value adjustments.

The swing from a $401M GAAP loss in FY2024 to $81M GAAP profit in FY2025 (+$482M improvement) demonstrates the operating leverage in the model. Once the fixed-cost base (AI R&D, platform infrastructure, corporate overhead) is covered, incremental FRLPC drops through at very high rates. Management guided FY2026 GAAP net income of $100-150M, implying continued profitability expansion.

### 5. The AI Network Model — Partners, Not Borrowers

Pagaya's competitive moat is its B2B positioning. Unlike Upstart (which operates a lending marketplace consumers interact with directly) or Affirm (which is consumer-facing POS), Pagaya embeds within existing lending partners' workflows. The company has 31+ partners including SoFi, LendingClub, Prosper, U.S. Bank, Ally, Klarna, and Visa. This B2B model creates several advantages:

1. **Low customer acquisition cost**: Pagaya does not pay to acquire borrowers — the lending partner does.
2. **Sticky integrations**: API-level integration creates switching costs; once embedded, partners rarely remove the platform.
3. **Diversification**: Volume is spread across 31+ partners, reducing concentration risk.
4. **Scalability**: Adding a new partner brings an entire origination pipeline without proportional cost increases.

### 6. Credit Cycle Sensitivity — The Double-Edged Sword

Pagaya's business is inherently tied to consumer credit conditions. In a benign credit environment (low unemployment, falling rates), more applications flow through partners, AI approval rates are higher, ABS investors demand less spread, and FRLPC margins expand. In a deteriorating environment, the reverse occurs — and Pagaya's retained interests in ABS trusts could suffer impairments.

The FY2024 GAAP loss of $401M was partly driven by fair value adjustments on retained interests during a period of elevated consumer delinquencies. As credit conditions normalized in 2025, these marks reversed and contributed to the $81M GAAP profit. Investors should understand that GAAP earnings will be volatile around credit cycles, even as Adjusted EBITDA (which strips out fair value marks) shows a smoother trajectory.

### 7. The Funding Channel Evolution

Pagaya's economic model depends on its ability to efficiently fund the loans its AI approves. The funding channels have evolved:

- **ABS securitizations**: Pagaya was the #1 personal loan ABS issuer in FY2023. ABS provides term funding but requires warehousing loans on the balance sheet pre-securitization.
- **Forward flow agreements**: Committed purchase agreements with institutional investors (e.g., Castlelake: $2.5B personal loans, $500M auto). These reduce balance sheet exposure and provide more predictable economics.
- **Revolving structures**: In 2025, Pagaya launched revolving ABS structures backed by personal loans, providing more capital-efficient funding.

The shift from on-balance-sheet to off-balance-sheet funding is a key de-risking narrative. As forward flow and revolving structures grow as a share of total funding, Pagaya moves closer to a pure fee-based model with lower capital requirements.

### 8. Tax Situation — Israeli Incorporation

Pagaya is incorporated in Israel and headquartered in New York. As an Israeli company, it benefits from Israel's innovation-friendly tax regime. The company has accumulated net operating losses that shelter near-term taxable income. The effective tax rate in FY2025 was approximately 15% on GAAP pre-tax income. As the company scales profitability, the blended effective tax rate is expected to normalize toward 18-22%, reflecting a mix of Israeli and U.S. tax obligations.

## Growth Strategy / Key Opportunities

### 1. Network Volume Expansion Across Verticals

Pagaya currently operates in three verticals with distinct growth trajectories:

| Vertical | Est. FY2025 Volume | % of Total | TAM | Penetration |
|----------|-------------------|-----------|-----|------------|
| Personal Loans | ~$6.3B | ~60% | ~$200B | ~3.2% |
| Auto Loans | ~$2.6B | ~25% | ~$600B | ~0.4% |
| Point-of-Sale | ~$1.6B | ~15% | ~$150B | ~1.1% |

Auto and POS are earlier in their growth curves. Auto, in particular, benefits from recent partner additions (Westlake Financial, Exeter Finance, top-4 auto captive) and the Castlelake $500M forward flow agreement. Management has guided FY2026 network volume of $11.25B-$13.0B (7-24% growth), with the midpoint implying ~15% growth.

### 2. FRLPC Margin Expansion

The FRLPC margin has expanded from 3.2% (FY2023) to 4.2% (FY2024) to 4.9% (FY2025). Drivers include:
- Improved AI model accuracy (lower default rates = better ABS execution)
- Shift toward forward flow agreements (lower warehousing costs)
- Scale efficiencies in production costs
- Potential for selective pricing power as value-add to partners becomes more evident

Our model assumes the FRLPC margin stabilizes in the 4.0-5.0% range, with the base case at 4.5%. While the FY2023-2025 trajectory showed rapid expansion, we believe 5.0% represents a ceiling given competitive dynamics and mix effects as newer, lower-margin verticals (auto, POS) grow as a share of volume.

### 3. New Lending Partners and Geographies

Pagaya added 4 major partners in FY2023 and has continued adding partners through 2024-2025, reaching 31+. The TAM of potential partners in the U.S. alone (thousands of banks, credit unions, and fintechs) is vast. Additionally, Pagaya has begun exploring international expansion, which could provide a long-term growth runway.

### 4. Asset-Light Transition

The strategic shift from retained interests to forward flow and revolving structures reduces balance sheet risk and capital requirements. This transition, if sustained, could re-rate the stock from a "fintech lender with balance sheet risk" to a "pure-play AI platform" — a distinction that carries significant multiple implications (compare Pagaya's ~2.6x EV/EBITDA to Upstart's ~13x or Affirm's ~16x).

### 5. Real Estate Vertical Optionality

Pagaya has publicly discussed expanding into mortgage/real estate as a future vertical. Given the $2T+ annual mortgage origination market, even modest penetration could be transformative. This is not in our base case but represents meaningful upside optionality.

## DCF Valuation Methodology

### Approach

We use a 5-year Discounted Cash Flow with a Gordon Growth terminal value. Key parameters:

| Parameter | Value | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| WACC | 13.0% | CAPM: 4.3% risk-free + 5.94 beta (high) x 5.5% ERP, blended with cost of debt. Given the extreme reported beta, we cap WACC at 13% as a reasonable risk premium for a profitable fintech. |
| Terminal Growth | 3.0% | Reflects long-term nominal GDP growth; consumer lending scales with economic activity |
| FCF Definition | Adj EBITDA - Cash Taxes - Capex - Change in Retained Interests | Captures the capital intensity of the balance sheet |

## Key Risks

**Credit cycle deterioration.** Pagaya's economics are fundamentally tied to consumer credit performance. A recession that increases delinquencies and defaults would compress FRLPC margins, impair retained interests, and potentially cause partner volume pull-backs. The FY2024 GAAP loss of $401M illustrates how credit-sensitive the P&L can be.

**Balance sheet exposure.** With $945M in loan and securities investments, Pagaya carries meaningful credit risk. While the asset-light transition reduces this over time, a sudden credit shock could require additional provisioning or capital raises that dilute shareholders.

**Partner concentration.** While Pagaya has 31+ partners, volume may be concentrated among a few large partners (e.g., SoFi, LendingClub). Loss of a major partner could meaningfully impact network volume.

**Dilution.** The gap between basic shares (82.5M) and diluted shares (102M) reflects ~24% potential dilution from exchangeable notes, RSUs, and options. Continued equity compensation could erode per-share value.

**Regulatory risk.** As an AI-driven credit decisioning platform, Pagaya faces regulatory scrutiny around fair lending, algorithmic bias, and consumer protection. Changes in regulations (e.g., requiring explainability in AI underwriting) could increase compliance costs or constrain the business model.

**High beta / volatility.** PGY's beta of 5.94 implies extreme price volatility. The 52-week range of $8.50-$44.99 (a 5.3x spread) reflects the market's uncertainty about the company's trajectory. This volatility is itself a risk for investors with shorter time horizons or margin constraints.

**Interest rate sensitivity.** Higher rates reduce consumer borrowing demand and increase Pagaya's own cost of debt (8.875% on the senior notes). Conversely, rate cuts are a tailwind — making the current environment (with potential cuts on the horizon) favorable.

**Terminal value concentration.** PV of Terminal Value is 69% of Enterprise Value in the Base case. While this is lower than many growth-stage companies, the valuation remains sensitive to long-run assumptions.

## Conclusion

Pagaya Technologies presents a compelling risk/reward at $11.49 per share. The company has crossed a critical inflection point — achieving four consecutive quarters of GAAP profitability, generating $239M in operating cash flow, and guiding for $100-150M in FY2026 GAAP net income. The AI platform model, embedded in 31+ lending partners, creates recurring fee revenue with operating leverage: each dollar of incremental network volume generates high-margin FRLPC with minimal variable cost.

The Base case implies $22.10/share — approximately 90% above the current price. The Bear case, which assumes near-zero volume growth and margin compression, implies $9.70 — modestly below the current price. The Bull case reaches $38.50. The risk/reward remains attractive: the Base case implies nearly a double, while even the Bear case suggests limited downside from current levels.

The most likely catalyst for re-rating is the continued demonstration of GAAP profitability and the strategic transition toward an asset-light model. As the market gains confidence that Pagaya's retained interest portfolio is manageable and that the AI platform is genuinely scalable, the valuation gap relative to peers (UPST at 13x EBITDA, AFRM at 16x, vs. PGY at 2.6x) should narrow meaningfully.

The right mental model is not "small-cap fintech lender with reverse-split baggage" but rather "profitable AI platform with 30+ blue-chip lending partners, $10.5B of facilitated volume, expanding margins, and a pathway to $500M+ in annual free cash flow — trading at less than 3x EBITDA because the market hasn't caught up to the profitability inflection."
